Содержание

Пособия серии Macmillan Exam Skills for Russia и Macmillan Exam Skills for Russia: Speaking and Listening обеспечивают подготовку учащихся к сдаче Единого государственного экзамена (ЕГЭ) по английскому языку.

Test 7

B1

1:D 2:С 3:A 4:F 5:G 6:E

Tapescript — B1

Speaker 1: My parents both worked when I was young, so they were out of the house quite a lot. I used to be looked after by my grandma and granddad. They would take me out to the park and we had a lot of fun, and then my parents would pick me up when they finished work. I was a very happy child, and I still have a great relationship with them today.

Speaker 2: I had to work really hard to make friends because we had to move house quite often. Dad was in the army, and they would send him to another job and we’d all have to follow him. It’s not easy suddenly to arrive in a new place when you’re eight, or eleven, or thirteen, but we managed. My sister was quite shy and I think it was harder for her.

Speaker 3: You wouldn’t think it to look at me now, but I was a real city kid. I love the farm, and my own children love it too, but it’s nothing like my life back then. We lived in a block of flats near a motorway. I never dreamed that one day I’d be the sort of person who likes looking after animals. I haven’t been back and I wonder sometimes whether it’s changed. I know I have.

Speaker 4: Until I was about eighteen, we lived next door to a garage and it was really noisy and smelly. There were always lots of cars around, and I used to wish that we lived in the countryside because I hated the smell of oil and petrol. It was close to Mum’s work, though, so we couldn’t really move. I was glad when I finally went away to university and found somewhere else to live.

Speaker 5: There’s a supermarket there now, but when I was little there were a few houses and a small park there. We used to play in the park and we had really nice neighbours. They knocked the houses down about ten years ago. It’s strange to think that those places only exist in my memory now. I sometimes think about those days when I visit that supermarket.

Speaker 6: Well, of course, I went to a boarding school, which meant that I slept at school, staying there even at weekends, and only went home in the holidays. I quite enjoyed it, to be honest. I had lots of friends there, and there was lots to do. But I do remember missing my family at times — well, I rarely saw them when I was a child, really.

Salesman: Good morning, madam.

Woman: Ah, hello. It’s in the kitchen. Just go round to the back door, would you?

Salesman: Well… I… erm…

Woman: Oh. Sorry. I thought you were the plumber. I’ve been having a problem with my washing machine. Er… what did you want, Mr… er…

Salesman: Winterbottom’s the name. Irving Winterbottofn, at your service. Now today could be your lucky day, madam. I’m in your area for today only with some very special offers, the kind of offers you won’t find in the supermarkets. Now, may I ask you a question, madam? Do you own a vacuum cleaner?

Woman: I… er… well… yes, I do… but what’s that got to do with…

Salesman: That’s good, because I’m not here today to sell you a vacuum cleaner.

Woman: Aren’t you?

Salesman: Oh, no, madam. Let me ask you this. How long do you spend getting your house into such lovely condition? Three hours a day?

Woman: Oh, goodness me, no. I spend about ninety minutes a day on housework.

Salesman: How would you like to make that just thirty minutes?

Woman: I don’t know… I suppose it would be better, but…

Salesman: Your waiting is over. Let me present the Wonder Cleaner. The Wonder Cleaner doesn’t just clean your home, it cares for your home. Through the use of static electricity, it attracts the dirt and dust in an instant! For just £30, or £35 for the deluxe model, you too can own a Wonder Cleaner.

Woman: I don’t think so. These things never work, do they?

Salesman: I assure you, madam, that the Wonder Cleaner works and I’m willing now at no extra cost to give you a demonstration of its amazing ability to…

Woman: No, no, thank you. That won’t be necessary. I’ll tell you what, though. I do need a new duster.

Salesman: Look no further, madam. I have with me today the Super Duster, available for a special price of £7, or the ordinary Dust Master for £5.

Woman: Well, I find that cheaper dusters don’t do the job properly, so I’ll pay the extra two pounds and take the Super Duster, please.

Salesman: You won’t regret it, madam. Now, can I interest you in a selection of cotton tea towels?

Tapescript — A8-A14

Situation 1

Woman: This isn’t the first time, is it? You go out and ask. me if I want anything, so I tell you to get some milk. Half an hour later, I’m still waiting until finally I have to go and get some myself. You come back half an hour after that and put the milk down on the table without saying a word. You should have said it was going to take you so long. That way, I could have gone myself in the first place! I’m very disappointed with you, young man.

Situation 2

Man: I went to see that new film with Vivian McDougal in it yesterday. Like most films these days, the action scenes were very loud, but I just feel like I’ve seen one too many of them. It seems like just another bank robbery movie until you get to the last five minutes, and I suppose the ticket’s worth it for that alone. I won’t give it away, but the characters aren’t what they seem. You should go and see it if you’ve nothing better to do.

Situation 3

Girl: I saw it in a magazine and it looked gorgeous! I bet you’ve had loads of people asking for it. I’ve had enough of it being on my shoulders all the time and I think it’s time for a complete change. Don’t go over the top, though — I don’t think my parents would let me get away with a pink fringe, for example. Just make sure it’s all the same length, quite short, and put blonde bits in. There’s the magazine I saw it in. Let me show you.

Situation 4

Man: It was last week. I was driving along and suddenly the car started making a funny noise, you know, like a Hunk, clunk’kind of noise. Well, I’m afraid I’m hopeless when it comes to these things so I took it to a garage. I was worried it might be a big thing and then he said it was going to cost over five hundred pounds! I had no way of knowing whether that was right or not, so I just told him to do it. I’m so useless! Maybe doing a course in mechanics might help save me some money!

Situation 5

Girl: It’s the way she just walked into my room without knocking that annoyed me. She wanted to borrow some money to go out with her friends. So I’m doing my homework when I suddenly hear a voice behind me saying:’Got any money?’ Well, I was furious because it’s not the first time she’s come in without permission. I started shouting at her and threw her out. In fact, I did have some cash, and she could have borrowed it, even though I don’t understand why she wants to go out with that group of people. She just has to learn to knock next time.

Situation 6

Man: Ah, officer. Yes. Well, you see, the thing is I left the hospital just a few minutes ago to go home. We had a bit of an emergency and had to rush there. Anyway, what with all the panic I took a wrong turning coming back and found myself in an unfamiliar part of town and in the middle of a one-way system. And the road signs just here aren’t very clear, so that’s how it happened.

Situation 7

Boy: Newspiece it’s called. It’s quite good. It’s a children’s programme, and what they do is, each week at the end of the show they ask viewers to suggest subjects for next week’s show. The subjects have to be things in the news that week, and the show’s presenters go off and research them, you know, get interviews, that kind of thing. It’s a good way to get children involved in the news, I think.

Tapescript — B1

Speaker 1: Yes, I’ve basically decided what I want to do when I grow up. I really want to be a teacher in a primary school. It must be so satisfying teaching young kids to read and write and seeing them start to learn about maths and science. I know it’s hard work, and the pay’s not very good, but there are more important things than money, aren’t there?

Speaker 2: I’d really like a job where I’m going on business trips all the time. You know, going to other countries and places I haven’t been to before and staying in really nice hotels. It sounds really exciting, doesn’t it? Lots of people have jobs like that these days — and some of them earn quite a lot of money too — so I think it’s possible that I’ll be able to get a job like that when I’m older.

Speaker 3: Well, my uncle’s a travel agent. He’s got ’ a shop in the High Street and I often help him out at weekends. I’m learning a lot about the business — it’s really interesting — and I think it’s something I’d like to do too when I’m older. I know my uncle will be pleased if I follow in his footsteps, and I can’t actually imagine myself doing anything else, to be honest.

Speaker 4: I’d hate to have a job where you go to an office and sit at a desk all day. In fact, I don’t really want to have a job at all! I really enjoy studying — I know it sounds strange — so when I leave school, I’m going to go to university to do a degree. If I do well, and I hope I will, then I’ll stay on after my degree to do research. Who knows? I may be a professor one day!

Speaker 5: Well, I know it’s a bit of a dream, but I want to be a rock star in a really popular rock band! Everywhere I go people’ll rush up to me and ask for my autograph, and I’ll be on TV and in the newspapers all the time. It sounds really cool, doesn’t it? The problem is, I’m not sure I’ve got any talent. Maybe that doesn’t matter, though!

Speaker 6: I don’t really know what I want to do when I leave school yet, but I do know what I don’t want to do! I don’t want to get a job and do it for the next forty years! It would be so boring, wouldn’t it? So I think my plan is to do something for about ten years, and then do something totally different for the next ten years or so. I hope I’ll be able to have at least four careers before I retire!

Tapescript — A1-A7

Librarian: Hi. Can I help you?

Woman: Er, yes. I’ve just moved to the area and I was wondering what I have to do to use the library.

Librarian: Right. Well, you have to fill in this application form here. It’s quite easy. Just put your name, address and telephone number. And we will need to see some form of identification, just to prove you are who you say you are!

Woman: What kind of thing do you need? I haven’t got my passport with me, I’m afraid.

Librarian: Have you got an electricity hill or a phone hill on you? We just need something that’s got your name and address on it, really.

Woman: I’ve got a bank statement in my bag. Will that do?

Librarian: That’s perfect. So, once you’ve filled in the form, I’ll check the details on the bank statement, and then I’ll give you a library card. That’ll allow you to take books out of the library.

Woman: Are there any rules I need to know about?

Librarian: There are a few, yes. You’re only allowed to have three books out at any one time.

Woman: I see. And how long can I keep books out for?

Librarian: You can have each book for two weeks. After that, you have to pay a small amount. It’s fifty pence per week.

Woman: That sounds reasonable, I suppose. And, do you have a music section? In the library I used to go to they had lots of CDs which you could take out.

Librarian: Yes, it’s the same thing here. The music section is on the third floor. And, once again, you can only have three CDs at any one time, and can only have them for three weeks.

Woman: Sorry, I didn’t quite understand that. Can I have three CDs and three books, or is it three of every thing in total?

Librarian: Yes, sorry. I didn’t make that very clear, did I? It’s three things in total, so that might be two books and a CD, or two CDs and one book. Does that make sense?

Woman: Yes. They had exactly the same policy at my last library, too. That’s fine. So, I’ll just fill out the form now, shall I?

Librarian: Yes, please. If you don’t mind.

Tapescript — A8-A14

Interviewer: And now as part of our regular Monday morning look at some of the things people in the local community have been doing recently, we’re joined by Alan Dawson. Alan, hi. Thanks for coming in.

Alan: No problem.

Interviewer: Now, Alan, we’re going to be seeing you soon in a major Hollywood movie, is that right?

Alan: Well, yes and no, Gloria. I’m pretty sure I will be in the film, yes. But I don’t know whether you’ll notice me. I’m not one of the stars, you see. I was an extra.

Interviewer: Okay. Let’s start at the beginning. What’s the movie?

Alan: It’s called Battle of the Giants and it stars Jeff Piper and Celia McCullum.

Interviewer: And some of the scenes were shot in the local area, were they?

Alan: Well, put it like this. Most of it was done in Hollywood, but I think people who live round here will be able to spot a few local landmarks in the battle scenes!

Interviewer: Right! So how did you get involved in the movie?

Alan: About six months ago, I saw an advert in the local paper asking for extras. They said acting experience wasn’t important, you didn’t have to be particularly good-looking, you didn’t have to have any skills really — just a desire to be in the background of a film.

Interviewer: And you applied?

Alan: Yes, I thought it sounded really interesting. You know, apart from a couple of school plays when I was a kid, I’ve never done any acting and it’s not often a chance like this comes up, so I jumped at it.

Interviewer: And what happened after you applied?

Alan: For about three months, nothing. Then, one day, I got a phone call asking me if I was still interested. I said I was, so they asked me to come to an interview the following week.

Interviewer: That interview — was that some kind of audition?

Alan: Not really. I didn’t have to do any acting or anything like that and as soon as I turned up, it became clear that I could be in the film if I wanted to be. I think the real point of it was to take my measurements so they could make a costume for me!

Interviewer: And how long after that did you have to actually act in the film?

Alan: About two weeks later. I’m not sure I’d call it real acting, though, and I didn’t have any lines to learn. I’m in six or seven crowd scenes, just standing around really. And in one scene with a few other extras we had to run away from one of the giants.

Interviewer: Did you enjoy it?

Alan: Oh, it was great fun!

Interviewer: And have you seen the finished film yet?

Alan: No, it hasn’t been released yet. But as soon as it comes out at the cinema, I’ll be going.

Interviewer: I’ll make sure I see it too. I’ll be looking out for you!

Alan: I hope you spot me!

They always want to know what grades I get each day, and I find it quite stressful. I can’t get As all the time. but I don’t think they understand that.

Speaker 6: It’s strange — I look at the relationships some of my friends have with their parents and it’s terrible. It sounds like they argue all the time, they don’t respect each other, and they don’t do things together. But that’s not what my life’s like at all. My mum and dad have always given me enough freedom, and always respected my opinion. They’re always there when I need them too. I hope I’m like Mum and Dad when I have kids.

Builder: Hello. Mrs Horrocks? I’m Matt Riley from Riley’s Building Company. We spoke on the phone.

Mrs Horrocks: Oh, hello. Yes, I’ve been expecting you. The problem is the roof. Let’s go into the garden and I think you’ll be able to see what the problem is.

Builder: Is it this way?

Mrs Horrocks: That’s right. Let’s go round the side of the house.

Builder: Okay. Yes, a lot of people round here have had the same problem. Last week’s storm did a lot of damage and these old houses just can’t take high winds.

Mrs Horrocks: I thought I’d got away with it, but then I noticed the problem.

Builder: Yes, it’s easy to miss these things. Now, where is it?

Mrs Horrocks: If you lookup there, to the right of the chimney, you can see what’s wrong.

Builder: Yes. Some of the tiles are missing. I’ll only need a day or two to fix that.

Mrs Horrocks: Really? I thought it was a big job.

Builder: You’re lucky. Some other people have suffered a lot more damage.

Mrs Horrocks: Yes, I know. My neighbour’s house is in a terrible state.

Builder: Now, you said on the phone that you also wanted to talk about something else.

Mrs Horrocks: Yes. I’m thinking of knocking a wall down between the living room and dining room to create one large room. I wondered if you had any advice.

Builder: Well, before you make any decisions, you should draw a plan with the rooms on it. That way, you’ll get a better idea of what you want, because if you change your mind later, it could be quite expensive.

Mrs Horrocks: Yes, that’s a good idea. Should I speak to an architect?

Builder: My advice is that an architect isn’t really necessary for a job like this. You’re not changing the basic structure of the house. Let’s go inside now and I’ll give you an idea of how much it’s going to cost.

Mrs Horrocks: Right. It’s this way, through the kitchen.
